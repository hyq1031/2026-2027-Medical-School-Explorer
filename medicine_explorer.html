Key Concepts Before You Apply

How Multi-Offer Systems Work (UAC / QTAC / VTAC / SATAC / TISC)

Each admissions round, the centre (e.g. UAC for NSW) issues exactly one offer: the highest-ranked course on your preference list among the ones that actually selected you that round. It does not send you every offer you're eligible for.

This means a common source of confusion — "why didn't School X make me an offer even though they interviewed me?" — is often not a rejection at all. It's a preference-ordering issue.

Strategy: rank your true first choice lower until you've banked a safety offer in an earlier round. Then reorder your list (safety choice to the bottom, real choice to the top) so the next round's single offer becomes the one you actually want.

Bonded vs Unbonded Offers

Some rural-entry or waitlist offers (e.g. at UNSW, WSU) are bonded — they carry a return-of-service obligation to work in a rural/regional (Modified Monash Model 3–7) area for a set period after you qualify.

The rural-service period for some bonded places typically applies after specialist fellowship (around 18 months), not immediately after graduating — by which point many doctors have a family or a spouse's career that makes relocating difficult.

Rule of thumb: prefer an unbonded offer if you have the choice between comparable programs. Buying out a bonded place after the fact can cost well into six figures.

How to Read the Data Confidence Tags

Every school on this site carries a confidence badge in its detail view: Officially Verified means it was checked directly against that university's own admissions page. Third-Party Estimate means it came from prep-company aggregator sites, not the university itself. Unverified means it hasn't been independently researched yet.

Treat "Officially Verified" as reliable for this cycle, and double-check "Third-Party Estimate" / "Unverified" schools directly with the university before relying on them.
